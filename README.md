## Hard question: "Why keep two or three lines under SR 26-2 — isn't that just cost?"

This is the depth-probe. A sharp hiring MD may ask why Citi still runs separate validation
(2nd line) and internal audit (3rd line) now that SR 26-2 went principles-based and
non-binding, and even says validation quality depends on "rigor, not organizational
structure." The trap is defending "three lines" as if SR 26-2 requires it. It doesn't, and
never uses the phrase. Win by showing you know **where the requirement actually comes from.**

**One-breath answer:**
> "SR 26-2 governs *how* you manage model risk, not the firm's governance architecture. The
> separate lines come from the OCC Heightened Standards and Citi's consent-order obligations,
> not from SR 11-7 or SR 26-2. And SR 26-2 preserves the independence anyway, it just stops
> prescribing the mechanics."

**The five pillars (know these cold):**
1. **The lines don't come from SR 26-2.** They come from the **OCC Heightened Standards
   (12 CFR 30, Appendix D)** and Citi's **2020 consent orders**. Appendix D's Risk Governance
   Framework explicitly requires three functions: front line units, independent risk
   management, internal audit. SR 26-2 softening model-risk *methodology* doesn't touch that.
2. **SR 26-2 keeps the independence, just drops the label.** Section VI flags conflicts of
   interest "between different reporting lines, such as model development and validation
   groups," and preserves a distinct audit role: audit "would generally not duplicate...
   validation... internal audit's role is generally to evaluate whether the model risk
   management practices are rigorous and effective." That IS the separation, described
   functionally.
3. **"Rigor not org structure" cuts the other way for Citi.** That line is flexibility for a
   *small* bank. For a $30B-plus G-SIB, the materiality logic pushes toward *more* independent
   assurance on high-risk systems, not less. Citi's AI is high-materiality.
4. **GenAI is out of SR 26-2 scope (footnote 3), which strengthens the case.** For the exact
   systems this role governs, there's no prescriptive floor, so an independent audit line is
   *more* necessary, not less.
5. **Someone must be able to audit the validators.** A 2nd-line validator can't provide
   independent assurance over their own function. The Audit Committee needs a line outside
   model risk entirely. Structurally impossible without a distinct 3rd line.

**Heightened Standards = universal, NOT a Citi quirk.** They apply to *every* OCC-supervised
bank with $50B+ in assets (JPM, BofA, Wells, USB, Citi, all of them). So you're defending
standard large-bank governance, not a one-off. Keep this distinction crisp:

| | Applies to | Nature | Duration |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Heightened Standards (12 CFR 30, App D)** | *All* large banks ($50B+) | Permanent category rule | Ongoing, doesn't expire |
| **2020 consent order** | *Citi only* | Enforcement action | Lifts when regulators satisfied |

## Hard question: the dual mandate, cost, and the consent order

If you raise (or she asks about) using "build with AI" to cut cost as the consent order winds
down, split two things that are easy to conflate:

- **Structural independence = permanent.** From the Heightened Standards, survives the consent
  order. AI can't *be* the independent line; independence is organizational separation and
  accountability to the Audit Committee, not whether a human or a tool runs the test. A tool
  built and run inside the function can't independently assure itself.
- **Cost of delivery = flexible.** Two real costs come down: consent-order remediation overhead
  (relaxes when the order lifts) and cost-per-unit-of-assurance ("build with AI" automates
  evidence gathering, testing, reporting).

**The MD-level flip:** as the order winds down, the AI risk surface is *exploding*, not
shrinking. So freed capacity + AI automation isn't "shrink the function to save money," it's
"hold cost roughly flat while coverage scales to a growing AI footprint." The win is avoiding
*linear headcount growth* as models proliferate. And building AI *into* audit creates new
things to audit (the tools themselves), so it shifts the work, never zeroes it.

**Closing script:**
> "The dual mandate is really about flexibility. The independent audit line is fixed, that's
> Heightened Standards, not the consent order, so it doesn't go away when the order lifts.
> What's flexible is the cost of delivering that assurance. Build-with-AI lets me automate the
> audit labor so the function scales to Citi's exploding AI footprint without linear headcount
> growth. As remediation-era overhead comes off, I reinvest that capacity into broader AI
> coverage rather than banking it as savings. Leaner unit cost, wider coverage, same independence."

**Consent-order status (be current, don't state an end date):** It lifts only when the OCC and
Fed decide, not on a calendar. As of early 2026 the two core 2020 orders remain in effect; the
OCC removed the July 2024 amendment in Dec 2025 (progress), and Citi is targeting completion in
2026 but it's the regulators' call. If asked: *"They lift when the regulators are satisfied.
Citi's targeting this year and had an amendment removed in December, but that's their call."*
